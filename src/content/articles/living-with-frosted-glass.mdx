## Living With Frosted Glass for a Few More Days
A few days ago, I wrote about how much I enjoyed the new Frosted Glass effect in the latest COSMIC desktop.

And I still do.

Visually, I think it's one of the nicest additions System76 has made recently.

But after using it as my primary desktop every day, I started noticing things that simply weren't obvious during the first few hours.

This isn't a correction to my previous article.

It's what happened after the honeymoon phase.

## The Desktop feels...Slightly Heavier 
The first thing I noticed wasn't dramatic.

Nothing crashed.

Nothing became unusable.

Instead, the entire desktop felt just a little less effortless.

Window animations weren't as snappy as before.

Opening the launcher sometimes felt like it took a fraction longer.

Switching workspaces wasn't as fluid as I remembered.

If you've never used the desktop without Frosted Glass, you probably won't notice.

But after months of using COSMIC before enabling it, the difference became surprisingly easy to feel.

It's subtle.

Yet it's there.

## CPU Usage Isn't Free
Blur effects aren't magic.

Every transparent surface has to sample whatever is behind it, blur those pixels, and redraw the result whenever something changes.

Menus.

Notifications.

Panels.

The launcher.

Quick settings.

Every one of these now requires additional rendering work compared to simply drawing an opaque rectangle.

When I monitored my system, I noticed CPU activity was consistently a little higher while interacting with the desktop.

Not dramatically.

Not enough to be a problem on modern hardware.

But definitely higher than before.

This isn't necessarily a flaw in COSMIC.

It's simply the cost of real-time compositing.

Beautiful interfaces almost always require more work from the system.

## Small Delays Add Up
The interesting thing is that none of the slowdowns are individually noticeable.

Instead, they accumulate.

You open notifications.

Open the launcher.

Switch workspaces.

Resize windows.

Open quick settings.

Each interaction feels perhaps one or two percent slower.

After hours of usage, that tiny difference becomes something your brain starts recognizing even if you can't immediately explain why the desktop feels slightly less responsive.


## Wallpaper Matters more than I Expected 
In my previous article, I mentioned that some wallpapers make the effect look better than others.

I still think that's true.

But I now realize it's a bigger limitation than I initially thought.

If your wallpaper is already soft or lacks contrast, the blur almost disappears.

If it's too colorful, menus can sometimes lose visual definition despite remaining readable.

Ironically, the feature that is supposed to improve aesthetics can sometimes make the interface feel less distinctive depending entirely on what's sitting behind it.

A good feature shouldn't rely this heavily on choosing the "right" wallpaper.

## It's Easier to Appreciate Than to Notice
This ended up being the most surprising realization.

During the first day, I constantly admired the Frosted Glass effect.

After several days, I barely noticed it anymore.

That's not because it became worse.

It's because our brains adapt remarkably quickly to visual improvements.

Meanwhile, the tiny responsiveness differences remained noticeable because they directly affect interaction rather than appearance.

The beauty faded into the background.

The performance characteristics did not.

## Would i keep it enabled 
Honestly...

I'm undecided.

If I'm showing someone COSMIC, Frosted Glass stays on.

It genuinely makes the desktop look modern, polished, and premium.

But when I'm spending an entire day writing code, reading documentation, and switching between dozens of windows, I find myself preferring responsiveness over aesthetics.

The difference isn't huge.

It's just enough that I notice it.

## Final Thoughts 
I don't think System76 made a mistake by adding Frosted Glass.

Far from it.

The implementation is tasteful, optional, and fits naturally into COSMIC's design language.

But after living with it for several days, I've come to appreciate the tradeoff it introduces.

Every visual enhancement has a cost.

Sometimes that cost is GPU time.

Sometimes it's CPU usage.

Sometimes it's battery life.

And sometimes it's simply the feeling that your desktop isn't quite as lightweight as it used to be.

Will I turn it back on occasionally?

Absolutely.

Will I leave it enabled all the time?

I'm not so sure anymore.

Well, I'd definitely appreciate someone who could help clear up this confusion for me.
