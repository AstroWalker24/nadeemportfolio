## Living With Frosted Glass for a Few More Days
A few days ago, I wrote about how much I enjoyed the new Frosted Glass effect in the latest COSMIC desktop. And I still do. Visually, I think it's one of the nicest additions System76 has made recently. But after using it as my primary desktop every day, I started noticing things that simply weren't obvious during the first few hours. This isn't a correction to my previous article. It's what happened after the honeymoon phase.


## The Desktop feels...Slightly Heavier 
The first thing I noticed wasn't dramatic. Nothing crashed. Nothing became unusable. Instead, the entire desktop felt just a little less effortless. Window animations weren't as snappy as before. Opening the launcher sometimes felt like it took a fraction longer. Switching workspaces wasn't as fluid as I remembered. If you've never used the desktop without Frosted Glass, you probably won't notice. But after months of using COSMIC before enabling it, the difference became surprisingly easy to feel. It's subtle. Yet it's there.

## CPU Usage Isn't Free
Blur effects aren't magic. Every transparent surface has to sample whatever is behind it, blur those pixels, and redraw the result whenever something changes. Menus, Notifications, Panels, The launcher, Quick settings. Every one of these now requires additional rendering work compared to simply drawing an opaque rectangle. When I monitored my system, I noticed CPU activity was consistently a little higher while interacting with the desktop. Not dramatically. Not enough to be a problem on modern hardware. But definitely higher than before. This isn't necessarily a flaw in COSMIC. It's simply the cost of real-time compositing. Beautiful interfaces almost always require more work from the system.

## Small Delays Add Up
The interesting thing is that none of the slowdowns are individually noticeable. Instead, they accumulate. You open notifications, open the launcher, switch workspaces, resize windows, or open Quick Settings. Each interaction feels perhaps one or two percent slower. Over hours of usage, those tiny delays add up until your brain begins to recognize that the desktop feels slightly less responsive, even if you can't immediately explain why.


## Wallpaper Matters more than I Expected 
I wrote something about wallpapers before. I said that some wallpapers make things look better than others.. I still think that is true.. Now I know it is a bigger problem than I thought. If your wallpaper is not sharp or is too plain the blur effect is hardly noticeable. If it has many colors the menus can be hard to see clearly even if you can still read them. It is funny that the thing that is supposed to make the desktop look nicer can actually make it look worse depending on the wallpaper you choose. A good feature should not need you to pick a kind of wallpaper for it to work well. The blur effect and the desktop should look good, with any wallpaper not some of them.

## It's Easier to Appreciate Than to Notice
This turned out to be the most surprising realization. During the first day, I found myself constantly admiring the Frosted Glass effect. After several days, however, I barely noticed it anymore—not because it had become any less impressive, but because our brains adapt remarkably quickly to visual enhancements. The subtle differences in responsiveness, on the other hand, remained noticeable because they affected how I interacted with the desktop rather than how it looked. The visual appeal gradually faded into the background, but the performance characteristics never did.

## Would i keep it enabled 
I'm still undecided.

If I'm showing someone COSMIC, Frosted Glass stays enabled. It genuinely gives the desktop a modern, polished, and premium feel. But when I'm spending an entire day writing code, reading documentation, and constantly switching between dozens of windows, I find myself valuing responsiveness more than aesthetics. The difference isn't dramatic, yet it's noticeable enough that I find myself paying attention to it.

## Final Thoughts 
I do not think System76 made a mistake by adding Frosted Glass. From it. The way they implemented Frosted Glass is tasteful entirely optional. It fits naturally within COSMICs design language.. After using it for several days I have come to appreciate the tradeoff that Frosted Glass introduces.

Every time you make something look better it costs you something. Sometimes it costs you some of the power of your graphics card. Sometimes it uses more of your computers processor. Sometimes it even takes away some of your battery life.. Sometimes it just feels like your desktop is not as fast or responsive as it used to be.

Will I turn Frosted Glass back on sometimes? Yes I will.

Will I keep Frosted Glass turned on all the time? I am not so sure about that anymore.

Maybe the answer really depends on what's most important to you. If you care about how things look then Frosted Glass is a choice. If you spend most of your time writing code reading documents and switching between applications you might like it better without Frosted Glass because it feels a little faster.

I am still not sure what to do.. I would really like to hear from anyone who has used Frosted Glass for a long time. If you have noticed the things or if your experience, with Frosted Glass has been completely different I would love to know what made you decide to use it or not use it.
